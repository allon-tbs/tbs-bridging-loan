Understanding Bridging Loans

What Is a Bridging Loan?

A bridging loan is short-term financing that covers a cash gap — the period between when you need funds and when your expected money arrives. The most common trigger is a property transaction: you need capital now, but your proceeds from a sale, an en bloc payout, or a business receivable haven't landed yet.

Unlike banks, which require confirmed sale proceeds before approving, Tembusu assesses the strength of your asset and your exit — and moves at the speed your situation demands.

When Do You Need a Bridging Loan?

  • Your property is sold but cash hasn't come in yet — and you need to move on your next purchase
  • En bloc payout delayed but new home purchase is due
  • Cash from a business deal or contract is on the way, but you need funds now
  • You want to exercise an OTP, but your current unit hasn't sold yet
  • You need to cover the cash-over-valuation (COV) on a new property purchase
  • You want to unlock cash from your property for a business or investment opportunity

TFS Advantage: Unlike bank bridging loans, which are TDSR-restricted and require you to hold a concurrent home loan with them, Tembusu's bridging finance stands independently — secured against your property, assessed on its merits.

Types of Bridging Loans

Which Structure Is Right for You?

Bridging loans come in two structures. The right one depends on your cash flow position during the loan period — here's how they compare.

Capitalised Interest Bridging Loan

Interest accrues over the loan tenure and is added to the principal — nothing is paid monthly. The full loan amount plus accumulated interest is repaid in one lump sum when your existing property completes its sale.

  • ✓No monthly repayments during the bridging period — preserves cash flow
  • ✓Simpler to manage alongside your new home loan obligations
  • ✗Total interest cost is higher as it compounds over the tenure

Simultaneous Repayment Bridging Loan

You service both your bridging loan and new home loan concurrently each month. Higher short-term outgoings, but interest is reduced as the principal reduces — lowering your overall cost of borrowing.

  • ✓Lower total interest paid over the loan tenure
  • ✓Principal reduces faster, shortening effective loan duration
  • ✗Higher monthly cash commitment while servicing two loans simultaneously
